Mutliple Linear Regression

Multiple linear regression (MLR), also known simply as multiple regression, is a statistical technique that uses several explanatory variables to predict the outcome of a response variable.

\[\hat{y}=b_0+b_1x_1+b_2x_2+\dots+b_nx_n\]

Standard regression equation

Beta weights

\[ z_{\hat{y}}= \beta_1z_{x_1}+\beta_2z_{x_2} \] \[\beta_1=\frac{r_{1y}-r_{2y}r_{12}}{1-r_{12}^2}; \beta_2=\frac{r_{2y}-r_{1y}r_{12}}{1-r_{12}^2}\] \[\beta_j=b_j\frac{s_Y}{s_j}\]

Correlation and Coefficient of Determination

\[R^2=\beta_1r_{1y}+\beta_2r_{2y}=\frac{r_{1y}^2+r_{2y}^2-2r_{1y}r_{2y}r_{12}}{1-r_{12}^2}\] F test for R \[F(P,N-P-1)=\frac{R^2/P}{(1-R^2)/(N-P-1)}\] ## Ajusted \(R^2\)

R sometimes increases with additional parameters in the model. The adjustment punishes the number of parameters.

\[R_{adj}^2=R^2-\frac{P(1-R^2)}{N-P-1}=R^2(1-\frac{1}{F})\]

Partial Correlation

The beta weights calculated for the standardized multiple regression equation are called standardized partial correlation coefficients.

The beta weight of a predictor is the multiple of SDs that the criterion (y) changes when that predictor (x) is increased by 1 SD, given that all other variables are held constant.

When your focus is on the relation between 2 particular variables (one is y, one is x in our case). However, for multiple correlation, there might be a number of variables (other x) that affect them both.

The pr between two variables is their correlation after holding a 3rd variable constant.

\[pr_1=\frac{r_{1y}-r_{2y}r_{12}}{\sqrt{(1-r_{2y}^2)(1-r_{12}^2)}}\]

Venn

\[pr_1=\frac{B}{A+B+C+D}\]

t test for pr

\[t(N-V-2)=\frac{pr\sqrt{N-V-2}}{\sqrt{1-pr^2}}\] V refers to the number of variables taken out

Semi-partial Correlation (Part Correlation)

The semi-partial correlation (sr) of a predictor with the criterion (y): the correlation between the criterion and that predictor after excluding the influence of all other predictors.

\[sr_1^2=R^2-r_{2y}^2=\frac{(r_{1y}-r_{2y}r_{12})^2}{1-r_{12}^2}\]

Venn

\[sr_1=\frac{B}{A+B}\]

Sample size

Minimum sample size

  • Normal distribution: \(N-P-1 >40\)
  • If $P, N10p $ or \(N\ge 15p\)
  • \(N\ge8P+50\)
  • \(30P \sim 40P\) (to reduce Type I error)

Multicollinearity

If a predictor can be perfectly predicted by other predictors, the correlation matrix is said to be singular, and a regression equation cannot be found.

Variance Inflation Factor (VIF)

The degree to which a predictor can be predicted by a combination of all other predictors in the model can be measured by tolerance or its reciprocal, the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF)

\[Tolerance=1-R_c^2\] \[VIF=\frac{1}{Tolerance}\] A high value for tolerance means that the predictor in question is relatively independent of the other predictors (tolerance <0.1 suggest serious multicollinearity, 0.2 suggests potential multicollinearity).

car::vif(fit)
##   biking  smoking 
## 1.000229 1.000229

Outliers

Cook distance: large values marks potential outliers.

plot(fit, which=4)

When both Cooks distance and leverage value are large, the observation is more likely an outlier.

plot(fit, which=6)

Residuals

Residuals vs Y_hat (fitted response): ideally they should be independent.

plot(fit, which=1)

QQ plot for standardized residuals: ideally they should be following y = x, indicating a Gaussian distribution underlies it.

plot(fit, which=2)

Key information to report for MLR

  • \(R_{adj}^2\):SS% explained?

    75%, very good ; 50-75%, good; 25-50%, OK; <25%, insufficient

  • F test: Significant association between IV (x) and DV (y)?

    H0: no association

  • B regression coefficient/slope: The relation between DV and each IV when keeping all other IV constant?

  • standardized beta: Which IV contributes most to DV?

    Standardized beta weights: z-scores of IVs and DV for regression

  • t: Significant association between each IV and DV?
